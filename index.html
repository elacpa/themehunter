"

How to approach this

Start by asking: does this work show two groups of people living completely different lives in the same world? Does someone in power fail to see what is happening around them? You are not looking for a perfect match — you are looking for the same underlying truth. Think across every class you have taken, every film you have seen, every song you know. The right work is already somewhere in your memory.

Paradox as a Tool

Your task

Find any work — any theme, any medium — where the artist uses paradox to carry their argument. The paradox should be the point, not just decoration.

"

Any genre, any era. The only rule: the paradox has to be doing real work — two opposite truths held at the same time, deliberately.

How to approach this

Remember what we said in class: a paradox is not a mistake — it is a choice. The artist is saying both of these things are real and I refuse to pretend otherwise. When you look at your chosen work, ask: what are the two opposite truths? Why could the artist not say this with just one side? Could they have resolved it, and did they choose not to? Your explanation should name both halves of the paradox and say what the tension between them is arguing.

⏱ 5 minutes

Pride & Prejudice

Two tasks this round

First: write the central theme of Pride & Prejudice as a whole — one specific sentence. Then find a work that carries that same theme and upload an image.

"
What a theme is — and is not

A theme is not a topic. A topic is one word. A theme makes an argument about that word.

  • "The book is about marriage" — that is a topic, not a theme
  • "Austen writes about class" — still just a topic
  • A theme takes a side. It claims something about the world.
  • A theme says something about what it means to be human.
Ask yourself: what is Austen actually arguing? What does she believe about freedom, survival, and what people owe themselves versus what society demands of them? What does the whole novel add up to?
How to approach this

Think about the whole arc of the novel — not just one character or one scene. Think about what happens to Elizabeth, to Charlotte, to Jane, to Lydia. What does the system do to each of them? What does Austen seem to be saying about that? Your theme sentence should make a claim that holds true across all of them — something Austen is arguing about the world, not just describing about the plot. Once you have your sentence, ask: what other story, song, or image makes the exact same argument?

❧

Not a summary. Not a topic. A claim about the world that Austen is making across the entire novel.
